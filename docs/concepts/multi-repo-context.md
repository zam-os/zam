# Multi-Repo Context: Life as Software Development

## The Personal Repository

A ZAM user begins with little knowledge, no defined goals, and no tooling. Over time, goals emerge and need a place to be stored, viewed, and edited. This place is a private source repository (e.g. on GitHub), co-owned by the human and the ZAM agent. They share one identity and operate under the same credentials.

Individual knowledge — preferences, beliefs, processes, automations — is persisted in this repo as soon as it is worth improving, automating, or partially automating. The description of how to do things can be in any language the agent can understand and write software for. A person starts with one way of doing things and refines it over time through learning. This is iterative development applied to life.

The user-agent pair selects tools and subscriptions to get things done efficiently. When an image is needed, it is generated by a tool, not hand-drawn and scanned. Resources can be acquired through subscriptions or one-time purchases.

## Goals and Involvement

Goals describe what a person wants to be able to do, or what resource they want to use or own. Examples:

- I want to be able to swim.
- I want to live long and healthy.
- I want a car big enough for my family. It should be a BMW.
- I want more green spaces in my town.
- I want a burger restaurant in my town.
- I want nobody in the world to starve.

Goals range from purely personal to world-scale. What differs is not the word used but the **level of involvement**:

- **Own**: You drive this goal. You are responsible for making it happen.
- **Contribute**: You invest partial effort. You print leaflets, submit code, volunteer time.
- **Vote**: You endorse the goal but contribute nothing beyond your support.

This maps to software development: a maintainer owns a feature, a contributor submits a pull request, a user upvotes an issue. A goal requiring change beyond your own repo is, in software terms, a pull request to someone else's repo — the townhall's, the employer's, or a community's.

Goals may need to be adapted over time. Not all parameters of a wish can be fulfilled immediately or ever. This is normal iteration.

## Repositories for Everything That Plans

Every individual, organization, company, community, town, country, or alliance that can think and plan should have a repository. Each repo describes the group's rules, goals, privileges, duties, processes, and services — as code. Processes are followed manually or executed automatically.

Group repos arise when individuals form constellations. They do not need to be long-lived. When a group dissolves, its repo enters a wind-down phase: resources are transferred, subscriptions end, and the repo is archived or deleted.

The same applies to individuals. When a person dies, the personal repo does not vanish instantly. It contains belongings and directives for inheritance. The repo becomes read-only: inheritance executes, subscriptions terminate, services cease. After the transition, the repo can be archived. No new commits will be made — the person will not provide services or set new goals.

## Communities and Membership

There are two kinds of community membership:

- **Location-based**: Your town, region, country — dictated by where you live.
- **Choice-based**: A church, a sports club, an open-source project, a professional association — joined because you share the group's worldview, want to receive a service, or want to provide one.

Service relationships (employer, telecom provider, etc.) are also memberships with defined terms: you provide or receive services in exchange for compensation.

## Access and Identity

The ZAM user and agent act under shared credentials. Access to repos follows standard scoping:

- **Personal repo**: Private. Only the user-agent pair has access.
- **Organization repos**: Accessible to members. You gain access by being an employee, citizen, or registered member.
- **Public repos**: Accessible to everyone. Even public repos may have private operational areas (internal workflows, automations) visible only to those with the appropriate role.

This mirrors existing access control: org membership, role-based visibility, public/private scoping. No special mechanism is needed — the agent inherits the user's access.

## Multi-Context Reasoning

When the ZAM agent reasons about how to reach a goal, it needs all relevant contexts: the individual's preferences and beliefs from the personal repo, plus the rules, constraints, and resources of the context in which the task is done — family, employer, town, community. All accessible repos become part of the agent's reasoning context. The agent's job is to navigate these contexts on behalf of the user-agent pair, respecting each repo's rules and the user's level of involvement.
